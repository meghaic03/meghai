Every retirement product was built for a world where people died at 73.

The 401(k) was created in 1978. Annuities were priced for shorter lives. Life insurance pays out at death. None of it was designed for the 25 years of life that now routinely follow retirement.

This is not a personal failure. It is a structural gap in the entire financial system.

The fear of running out changes how people live — long before the money actually runs out.

People stop spending in their 70s.

Research consistently shows that retirees underspend relative to their savings — not because they lack money, but because they are afraid of running out. They hold back to preserve a cushion against an unknown future.

Adult children become the safety net.

When savings run out at 86 and a parent lives to 95, the gap falls to family. Adult children drain their own retirement savings, delay their own plans, and carry the weight of a problem that was never theirs to carry.

Nothing is left to pass on.

The wealth families spent a lifetime building gets consumed in the final decade — on care, on daily expenses, on the cost of a life that outlasted its financial plan. What was meant to be passed down gets spent on staying afloat.
